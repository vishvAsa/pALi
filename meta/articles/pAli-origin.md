## Not mAgadhI
Pali does not descend from Old Magadhi. Pali is a liturgical language that is most likely based on a midland dialect such as the Prakrit of Avanti and the Sinhalese Buddhist use of "Magadhi" to refer to Pali was a misnomer.

You would be aware of the fact that Pali is closer to Shauraseni than Magadhi if you had actually read (and hopefully comprehended) Suniti Kumar Chatterji's "Origin and Development of the Bengali Language." It was based on a midland dialect, but the midland dialect in question must have been a dialect of a Western Prakrit similar to Śaurasenī or the Prakrit inscriptions at Girnar.

## Āvantī
Ujjayinī or Vindhya region seems to be the general consensus among scholars (Walpola Rahula is not a linguist). That's why I mentioned Avantī. In any case, it's clear that Pali is an artificial liturgical language that is based predominantly on a Western Prakrit and not an Eastern Prakrit.

Prākṛtakalpataru mentions the use of saḍiccha in Āvantī instead of sariccha for sadṛkṣa. Not quite the same as for Māgadhī (kṛñmṛṅgamāṃ ktasya ḍaḥ), but nonetheless retroflexion conditioned by ṛ rather than by "r." Mahārāṣṭrī also has the feature, albeit lexically limited.

## Minor mAgadhi influence
The Pali language itself is clearly based on a Western dialect, though there are rare Magadhicisms that may have survived from an earlier Māgadhī recension that existed prior to the Canon being translated into Pāli. They are even mutually intelligible to a considerable extent. Yet Pali predominantly shows features of a western Indo-Aryan language while the Pali canon itself has interspersed Magadhisms (or Magadhicisms?) like "vanappagumbe yathā phussitagge" which suggest that Buddha originally taught his sermons in an eastern Indo-Aryan language.



## Liturgicalness
I used the term "based on" because the Pali language as it currently stands was likely never a spoken language per se. Certain clusters in Pali are clearly ahistorical.

## Translation
The argument that "it is probably based on the Māgadhī that the Buddha generally spoke" is indeed very disagreeable and flies in the fact of the facts.

While it is true that Pali as an artificial "khichdi" language does not represent a single dialect, Pali is nonetheless quite close to the dialect of the Girnar edicts and closer still to the dialect of Avanti. It's not particularly close to any Eastern Prakrit dialect. Save for the cerebralization of dentals following ṛ (a feature also shared by Avanti Prakrit), there is hardly any reason for classifying Pali as an "Eastern" Prakrit. I think it is quite likely that an early canon in Ardhamāgadhī or Māgadhī was translated into Pali in Ujjain as the Ashokan inscriptions themselves mention Lāghulovāde while the current Pali canon has Rāhulovādo.

## Not deccan
If it originated in the Deccan, wouldn't one expect a greater proportion of loans from Dravidian languages?
While it's true that Pāḷi has a few Dravidian loans that don't occur in Sanskrit and vice versa, we don't find it Pāḷi to be as enriched with Dravidian loans as we see in Mahārāṣṭrī, e.g. ṇesara (sun, cf. Kan. nēsaṟu, Tam. ñāyiṟu), olaga (servant, cf. Kan. ōlaga, royal advisor), karaḍa (tiger, cf. Tam. karaṭi, bear), pullī (id, cf. Tam./Tel. puli, Kan. huli), jheṃḍu(y)a (=kanduka, ball, cf. Tel. ceṇḍu), goñjī/gocchā (bundle of flowers), etc.
The Pkt. word chāṇī/chāṇa for "cow-dung" is listed by lexicographers as "deśīya" and is possibly Dravidian origin, but I avoided mentioning it as I've also seen it derived from Skt. śakán-,  which, as you are probably aware, optionally takes the place of śákṛt as a śasādi suppletive, similar to pád- for pādá- (foot), dát- for dánta- (tooth), nás- for nā́sikā- (nose), mā́s- for mā́sa- (month), hṛ́d- for hṛ́daya- (heart), níś- for níśā- (night), asán- for ásṛj- (blood), yūṣán- for yūṣá- (broth), doṣán- for dós- (arm), yakán- for yákṛt- (liver), udán- for udaká- (water), and āsán- for āsyà (mouth).

Even the Sātavāhana king Hāla uses a few Dravidian words in Sattasaī which aren't found in Sanskrit such as attā (mother-in-law), *poṭṭa (belly), tuppa (ghee, or alternatively "red pigment/dye"?), etc. and these verses were likely composed in the Deccan only a few centuries after the compilation of the Pāḷi canon.
I suppose one could argue that the relative infrequency of Dravidian loans can be attributed to the infrequency of Dravidian loans in the original canon (composed in a different Prakrit language than Pāḷi), though the Pali canon is so massive that I'm reluctant to accept that argument.
P.S. I'm aware that the traditional attribution of Gāhāsattasaī to Hāla (whether it be as author or as compiler) is almost universally rejected nowadays.
I'm also aware that a few people have even argued that Hāla isn't the proper name of a ruler but rather a syncopated Prakrit form of the name of the dynasty to which he belonged (Hāla<Hāḷa<Hāḍagaṇi<Hātakaṇṇi<Sātakarṇi).
But regardless on one's position regarding authorship of the text, it's undeniable that the anthology in question (Sattasaī) represents one of the earliest extant works in Mahārāṣṭrī and hence serves as a good point of reference.
*Also attested as puṭṭa:

golāṇaīĕ kacche cakkhaṃto rāiyāi pattāiṃ
abbhiḍaï makkaḍo khukkhuei puṭṭaṃ ca piṭṭei
While savoring the mustard leaves on the banks/marshes of the river Godā (i.e. Godāvarī), the monkey leaps, emits a "kho kho" sound and strikes his belly.

TL;DR: The word tuppa (or some variant thereof) was likely borrowed into IA from Dravidian in the sense "reddish or rust-like dye" and was later borrowed back into Dravidian from IA in the sense "ghee."
